Overview

Planning a wedding shouldn't feel like work

LockedIn is a wedding planning mobile app designed to help engaged couples manage every detail of their big day in one place. From tracking the countdown to managing a guest list, from budgeting vendor costs to browsing catering and beauty options LockedIn brings calm and clarity to one of life's most exciting (and overwhelming) experiences.

The app is built around four core pillars: the home dashboard with countdown, a budget tracker, a guest manager, and a to-do system with vendor browsing. The visual design is intentionally soft and romantic white cards, muted teal, script typography to feel personal rather than transactional.

The Problem

Wedding planning is fragmented

Most couples juggle spreadsheets, notes apps, vendor emails, and physical checklists all disconnected. The mental load of keeping every detail straight becomes its own full-time job. Existing wedding planning tools are often cluttered, overwhelming, or feel impersonal.

LockedIn was designed to solve this by consolidating everything countdown, budget, guests, and vendor discovery into a single, visually calm app that feels as special as the occasion itself.

Design Process

From problem to prototype

Design Decisions

Why it looks the way it does

Countdown as emotional anchor
The home screen leads with the couple's names and a months/days/hours countdown over a warm photo. This makes every session feel meaningful you're not just managing tasks, you're counting down to something beautiful.
Soft teal as the signature colour
The muted teal used for headings, active states, and the nav bar is deliberate it's calm and sophisticated without being cold. It reads as trustworthy and wedding-appropriate across all screen types.
Budget donut chart
Rather than a wall of numbers, the budget screen leads with a donut chart showing total vs. spent at a glance. The remaining and paid cards below provide the detail information hierarchy that respects the user's attention.
Vendor photo grid in To Do
When browsing a category like Catering, vendors are shown as a photo grid with pill labels visual-first browsing that mirrors how people actually discover vendors (through imagery, not text lists).
Guest status at a glance
The Guests screen uses four clear status types Invited, Accepted, Declined, Reserved with distinct icons and a clean summary panel. No ambiguity about where every guest stands.
Beauty subcategory selector
The Beauty screen uses circle icon selectors (Dresses, Make Up Artist, Hair Salon, Fragrance) before showing options a step-by-step narrowing approach that reduces overwhelm when choices are plentiful.

Reflections

What I took away

  • Designing for emotion, not just function, produces better products. Every screen in LockedIn was considered not just for usability but for how it would make the user feel and that emotional consideration elevated the whole design.
  • Information hierarchy is everything in mobile. With limited screen real estate, I had to ruthlessly prioritise what appears first. The donut chart, the countdown, the photo grid each leads with the most emotionally resonant element, then provides detail below.
  • Consistency builds trust. The teal accent colour, the rounded white card shape, and the thin Lato body type appear on every screen creating a design system that feels intentional and reliable, even across very different content types.
  • Visual browsing beats text lists for discovery. The switch from a plain category list to a photo grid in the vendor section was the single most impactful design decision it transformed browsing from a chore into something enjoyable.
