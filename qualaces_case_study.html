Overview

A brand that earns trust

Qualaces is a software quality assurance company based in Calgary, Alberta. When I joined as their UX Designer, the company had strong technical expertise but lacked a visual identity that communicated that confidence to potential clients. The challenge was clear: build a brand and website experience from the ground up that would signal credibility, clarity, and partnership.

This project touched everything brand strategy, visual identity, information architecture, UI design, and developer handoff. I owned the design end-to-end across a seven-month engagement.

Homepage hero final high-fidelity design

The Problem

No visual language

Before this rebrand, Qualaces had no consistent visual identity. Their web presence didn't reflect the depth of their QA expertise and was failing to convert prospective clients who landed on their site. Stakeholder interviews revealed that even internal team members struggled to describe what made Qualaces distinctive.

The brief: create a visual system and website that communicates expertise, reliability, and approachability and do it in a way that's scalable as the company grows.

My Process

How I approached it

I followed a research-first, iterate-often process making sure design decisions were always grounded in what stakeholders and users actually needed, not just what looked good.

Visual Identity

Building the design system

The core design language was built around three pillars: expertise (deep purple conveys authority and intelligence), innovation (teal and cyan accents signal modernity and forward-thinking), and clarity (clean white space and bold typography ensure nothing is ambiguous).

Typography pairing: Inter for headings (bold, confident) with a friendly sans-serif for body keeping everything legible at every scale. The underline accent motif (used beneath key words in headings) became a signature detail that appears throughout the site to draw attention to what matters most.

Key Design Decisions

Why it looks the way it does

Diagonal section dividers. Rather than flat horizontal sections, I introduced diagonal cuts between page sections. This creates visual momentum and makes the page feel dynamic without being overwhelming. It became a key signature of the Qualaces visual language.

Purple-dominant, teal-accented. The deep purple was chosen deliberately to differentiate Qualaces from the typical blue-and-grey QA industry aesthetic. The teal accent adds warmth and a sense of innovation. Together they communicate both reliability and forward momentum.

Illustration style. I selected a consistent set of purple-toned illustrations showing people in professional, collaborative settings humanizing a technical company and making the brand feel approachable rather than cold.

Card-based service layouts. Each service type lives in its own card with a coloured icon, making scanning easy. This decision came directly from user research showing that prospective clients wanted to quickly match their specific needs to a service offering.

The final product was a fully responsive website that gave Qualaces a professional, distinctive presence and a design system the team could extend as the company grew. The collaboration model I set up with developers (regular check-ins, annotated specs, implementation reviews) ensured the design was built faithfully.

What I Learned

Reflections & growth

  • Stakeholder alignment early on saves enormous time later. My most impactful decision was spending the first few weeks entirely on research and definition before touching a single design tool.
  • Developer collaboration isn't a handoff it's a continuous dialogue. Some of my best design decisions came from conversations about what was feasible and what could be made even better in code.
  • Brand identity is as much about what you exclude as what you include. Saying no to design ideas that didn't align with the core brand pillars was a constant discipline and it made the final system much more cohesive.
  • A system designed for scalability from day one pays dividends. Building a reusable component library meant that adding new pages later required no rethinking just application.
